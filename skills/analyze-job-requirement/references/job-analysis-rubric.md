# Job Analysis Rubric

Use this rubric to analyze job descriptions without turning them into generic summaries.

## Source Assessment

Capture:

- Job title and level, including any mismatch between title and responsibilities.
- Company, department, product area, or business unit if stated.
- Location, remote/hybrid expectations, travel, schedule, contract type, and compensation if stated.
- Posting freshness or URL access date when using a live page.
- Missing or vague metadata that could affect candidate decisions.

## Responsibility Categories

Group responsibilities into these buckets when present:

- Core execution: recurring tasks and deliverables.
- Ownership: systems, accounts, projects, metrics, roadmaps, programs, or outcomes the role owns.
- Strategy: planning, prioritization, analysis, architecture, design, or decision-making.
- Collaboration: teams, stakeholders, customers, vendors, leadership, or regulators.
- Communication: reporting, documentation, presentations, training, or customer communication.
- Leadership: mentoring, management, review, coaching, hiring, or setting standards.
- Operations: process improvement, incident response, quality control, compliance, support, or administration.

Explain what the responsibility means in practice. Avoid simply rephrasing every bullet.

## Qualification Categories

Separate qualifications into:

- Required: explicit must-haves, minimum years, credentials, legal eligibility, language, location, security clearance, or mandatory tools.
- Preferred: nice-to-haves, bonus experience, industry exposure, advanced degrees, or differentiators.
- Implied: not stated as a requirement but reasonably suggested by responsibilities.
- Potentially negotiable: requirements that appear broad, inflated, or listed as preferred elsewhere.

## Knowledge And Skill Map

Identify knowledge requirements across:

- Technical skills: programming languages, systems, analytics, platforms, infrastructure, security, design tools, or operational tooling.
- Domain knowledge: industry concepts, customer segment, product domain, business model, regulations, or market context.
- Methodologies: agile, research, sales process, financial modeling, experimentation, compliance frameworks, project management, or service design.
- Tools: software, databases, CRMs, cloud providers, productivity suites, BI tools, ticketing systems, or specialized platforms.
- Soft skills: stakeholder management, written communication, ambiguity tolerance, leadership, negotiation, facilitation, customer empathy, or attention to detail.

## Seniority Signals

Infer level cautiously from:

- Scope of ownership: task execution, project ownership, program ownership, org-level influence, or strategy ownership.
- Autonomy: closely guided, independently owns work, defines approach, or sets direction.
- Stakeholders: peers, cross-functional teams, executives, customers, partners, or regulators.
- Ambiguity: clear backlog versus undefined problems and new operating models.
- Leadership: mentoring, leading projects, managing people, setting standards, or owning hiring.
- Experience depth: years required, complexity of systems, scale of impact, and breadth of tools.

Use labels such as entry-level, junior, mid-level, senior, lead/staff, manager, or executive only when supported by the posting.

## Evidence Rules

- Treat exact job-post language as evidence, not decoration.
- Quote only short phrases when they materially support a conclusion.
- Label inference plainly.
- Do not infer illegal or sensitive hiring criteria.
- Do not provide legal, immigration, or salary-negotiation advice beyond general informational framing unless the user asks and the answer can be properly sourced.
